1. Current Regulatory Standards (IS & National Building Code)

The framework of fire safety compliance in Indian industrial facilities is fundamentally governed by the National Building Code (NBC) of India (Part 4: Fire and Life Safety) alongside specific Bureau of Indian Standards (IS) specifications (e.g., IS 15105 for design of sprinkler systems, IS 2189 for automatic fire detection and alarm systems). While these provisions outline explicit technical dimensions for passive fire barriers and active suppression, their historical integration into operations has remained largely compliance-driven rather than performance-oriented.

Epidemiological and industrial health safety studies (Unnikrishnan et al., 2014; Behera & Hassan, 2019; Srinivas D., 2025) note that while regulatory inspections correlate broadly with reduced accident rates, the direct, isolated impact of fire audits is often obscured by systemic lapses in regular oversight. Research indicates that standard audit frameworks operate as static assessments, presenting a binary pass/fail condition that lacks predictive insight. Modern compliance architectures necessitate a transition toward a continuous risk calculation framework, quantified as:

$$R_f = \sum_{i=1}^{n} (P_i \times C_i) \times (1 - \eta_{audit})$$

Where $R_f$ is the residual operational fire risk, $P_i$ and $C_i$ represent the occurrence probability and consequence severity of specific hazard vectors, and $\eta_{audit}$ is the empirical safety assurance factor generated by active, integrated inspections.

2. Key Compliance Bottlenecks

Analyzing the operational realities of Indian manufacturing and fireworks facilities reveals persistent friction points. Gaps do not merely stem from a lack of written code, but from systemic deficiencies in execution, oversight, and internal performance tracking. Investigations (Fernandes & Mulimani, 2021) demonstrate that audits are frequently decoupled from internal corrective action pipelines, resulting in repetitive safety gaps that remain unresolved until an external audit cycle occurs (Hutchinson et al., 2024).

The Operational Indicator Imbalance

Traditional OHS models emphasize lagging indicators over active measures, exposing facilities to sudden risk failures before any mitigation triggers occur.

The Lagging vs. Leading Disconnect

Relying exclusively on lagging indicators (e.g., incident frequency, property damage costs, reported injuries) is fundamentally flawed for high-hazard industrial settings. As highlighted in standard OHS literature, lagging indicators are retrospective; they record failure only after loss occurs. In contrast, Safety Performance Leading Indicators (SPLIs) measure the health of proactive control systems (e.g., preventative maintenance intervals, emergency drills, employee certification levels) before an incident, providing early warning signals to mitigate hazards.

3. Data-Driven Safety Solutions

Transitioning to a highly effective risk mitigation model requires replacing isolated binary checklist audits with continuous, quantitative hazard evaluations. Research indicates that utilizing a safety portfolio model (comprising multiple tailored KPIs rather than a single metric) provides the necessary flexibility to adapt to unique site risks (Faveto et al., 2023).

01. Portfolio Optimization

Rather than tracking a single index, utilize structured frameworks that extract and prioritize critical KPIs customized for high-hazard areas (e.g., volatile chemical storage vs. general assembly lines).

02. Quantitative Fire Risk Scores

Establish algorithmic calculating models (Glukhanov, 2026) to derive individual fire-risk probability based on continuous parameter inputs (e.g., containment integrity, humidity profiles, equipment age).

03. Corrective Follow-through

Enforce mandatory execution loops where identified non-compliance points trigger automatic maintenance orders, solving the "lack of follow-through" gap noted in inspections.
